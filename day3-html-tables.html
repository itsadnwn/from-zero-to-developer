Day 3: HTML Tables (My Lego Moment)

13 July, 2025

You know that feeling when you're building with Legos and suddenly everything just clicks? That's exactly what happened when I finally understood HTML tables.

Even though I've understood the whole HTML as a body anatomy - each tag is an organ by itself and has its own function - I was still a little stumbled when it came to understanding the tables. More like, how things should be layered because there's the <th>, <tr>, <td> that I was a little confused about.

Then I started looking at the whole thing like building a Lego set.

The Foundation Brick

Just like every Lego build starts with the base plate, every table starts with <table>.

<table>
  <!-- Everything builds on top of this -->
</table>

Stacking The Rows

Then you start stacking your rows with <tr> - think of these as those long flat Lego pieces that run horizontally across your build:

<tr>
  <!-- This is one layer of your structure -->
</tr>

The Individual Blocks

Inside each row, you snap in your individual pieces - <td> for regular data blocks and <th> for those special header blocks (you know, like the different colored pieces that stand out):

<th>Special Header Block</th>
<td>Regular Data Block</td>

Building in Sections (The Game Changer!)

Here's where it gets really cool - just like you might build a Lego castle with distinct sections (foundation, walls, towers), tables have their own sections:

  • <thead> - This is like building the top most section with all your special header pieces
  • <tbody> - The main body where most of your blocks go
  • <tfoot> - The bottom section for totals or summary pieces
<thead>
  <tr>
    <th>Header 1</th>
    <th>Header 2</th>
  </tr>
</thead>

<tbody>
  <tr>
    <td>Data 1</td>
    <td>Data 2</td>
  </tr>
</tbody>

<tfoot>
  <tr>
    <td>Total</td>
    <td>100</td>
  </tr>
</tfoot>

My "Aha!" Moment

When I built my first properly structured table, it was like completing a Lego set for the first time - everything had its place and purpose:

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Game</th>
      <th>Platform</th>
      <th>Year</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>The Legend of Zelda: Echoes of Wisdom</td>
      <td>Switch</td>
      <td>2024</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Split Fiction</td>
      <td>PC</td>
      <td>2025</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

For visual purposes, the table looks like this:

Game Platform Year
The Legend of Zelda: Echoes of Wisdom Switch 2024
Split Fiction PC 2025

The Special Pieces: Rowspan and Colspan

Just like Lego sets have those special pieces that span across multiple connection points, HTML tables have <rowspan> and <colspan>. These let you make cells stretch across multiple rows or columns:

<td colspan="2">This cell spans 2 columns</td>
<td rowspan="3">This cell spans 3 rows</td>

It's like using those flat Lego pieces that connect across multiple studs - they help you create more complex structures that aren't just basic grid patterns.

What I Realized

The Lego analogy really helped me understand that each HTML table element is like a specific type of block with its own purpose. You can't just randomly throw them together - there's a system. Just like you wouldn't use a tiny 1x1 block where you need a long base piece, you wouldn't use <td> where you need <th>.

And just like Legos give you that satisfying rigid structure that holds everything together, HTML tables give your data that same kind of reliable framework. Whether it's the skeletal system holding your body up or Lego blocks holding up your creation, it's all about having the right structure in the right place.

The best part? Once I got the hang of it, building tables became as intuitive as snapping Lego pieces together!
